# Sering Dictionary — v0

**Date:** 2026-05-15
**Status:** Seed vocabulary. Three phrases. Add slowly.

---

## What this is

A small, deliberate set of phrases that capture how we work at Sering. Each one functions on two levels: a literal kitchen instruction that anyone can use, and an underlying principle about how we run a place.

These phrases are meant to be **said out loud**. Across the bar, mid-prep, in passing. They are how we talk to each other. The serious meaning lives underneath the literal meaning; both stay alive as long as the phrase keeps getting used.

## Rules for the dictionary

- Phrases earn their place by being reached for unprompted, in multiple contexts, by people who weren't told to use them.
- Each phrase should work at face value as a real instruction *and* carry a deeper principle. If it only works as philosophy, it doesn't belong here — it belongs in a chunk.
- Phrases are added carefully and almost never removed. The dictionary is a thing that survives leadership turnover. Stability is part of how it does its job.
- A new phrase has to hold up next to the existing ones. Same register, same kind of compression, same ability to be used in jest.
- Three is a real number. Don't pad it.

## The phrases

### If the basics are right, the rest will follow

*Get the foundation correct and the upper layers grow from it. Try to skip the foundation and everything above it falls apart.*

**Literal:** Before you reach for the special technique, the creative plating, the warm interaction with a guest — make sure the basic things are in place. The pan is hot enough. The mise en place is set. The water is on the table. The room is clean. These are not interesting; they are non-negotiable.

**As a way of working:** Most failures at Sering are not failures of skill or creativity. They are failures of foundation. Dishes go wrong because something basic was skipped; service goes wrong because the room wasn't ready before guests arrived; a project stalls because the underlying systems weren't set up well. The fix is almost never "be more clever." It is "go back and fix the basics first."

**In daily speech:** Used when someone is reaching for sophistication before they've earned it. *"If the basics are right, the rest will follow"* is the gentle correction. Said to yourself when you notice you're elaborating on something before the foundation is set. Said to a chef who's planning a fancy garnish on a sauce that isn't seasoned right. Said to a shift lead trying to manage the room before the basics of the room are set.

---

### Always be closing

*Every action you take should be made with the future close in mind. You are never just doing the thing in front of you; you are doing it in relation to all the moments that come after.*

**Literal:** As you work, think about closing. Stack pans in a way that makes them easier to wash. Wipe as you go. Don't create work that someone (probably you, probably tired) will have to undo at 11pm. Set down the cutting board in a way that makes the next motion easier. Plan the prep so the kitchen ends the night clean.

**As a way of working:** Your job is not this moment. Your job is this moment *in relation to all the moments that follow*. Care for future-you and future-others is built into every small choice in the present. The shift that ends well wasn't built in the last hour; it was built in every small motion across the night. This applies beyond closing a kitchen — it applies to closing an event, closing a season, closing a project. Always be thinking about how this ends.

**In daily speech:** Said in jest when someone is creating future work for themselves. *"Always be closing!"* yelled across the kitchen when someone leaves something out that they'll have to deal with later. Said seriously when planning a long day or a hard shift: *we're going to think about close from the start.* A central phrase for shift leads especially.

---

### Keep your station clean

*Your station, your process, and your head are the same kind of thing. Clean is a discipline that runs through all three.*

**Literal:** Wipe your cutting board. Cap the sauces. Put your knives back where they belong. Don't let your area become a small zone of chaos in someone else's clean kitchen. A clean station is faster, safer, and visible to everyone walking past — including you.

**As a way of working:** The same habit that keeps your station clean keeps your processes clean and your mind clean. *Mise en place* is not just where your ingredients are; it's how you organize your work, your attention, your day. Someone who can't keep their station clean usually can't keep their processes clean either — sloppiness is correlated across levels, and so is discipline. Keep yourself *mis en place*. Don't carry yesterday's argument into today's shift. Don't run three half-things at once. Don't leave half-done work in your head the way you wouldn't leave it on your bench.

**In daily speech:** The most yell-able of the three. *"Keep your station clean!"* shouted across the kitchen with a grin when someone is daydreaming during prep, or when someone's bench has become a disaster, or when someone's mind is clearly elsewhere. Funny, true, and slightly philosophical all at once. Used in jest constantly; the serious meaning is preserved by the constant use.

## What's not in here yet

The dictionary is small on purpose. The hospitality chunk surfaced several other candidate phrases that may eventually graduate:

- *The basis is good and silent.*
- *We are above the squabble.*
- *You are the one at home here.*
- *Don't be everything; be present.*
- *Always work from the bottom up.*
- *Good work deserves to be done well.*

These will earn their place — or not — through use. If a senior staff member finds themselves reaching for one of them unprompted, in a context other than the chunk where it appeared, that's evidence it's traveling. If a phrase only ever appears when reading the chunk that contains it, it's not earning its place and stays out.

## How phrases get added

When drafting a chunk, note phrases you reach for from your existing vocabulary, especially ones that come from outside the chunk's domain. Phrases that travel from cooking into hospitality, or from closing into management, are the strongest candidates.

A phrase is ready for the dictionary when:

1. It's been used unprompted in at least two different contexts by at least two different people
2. It works at face value as a literal instruction
3. It carries a deeper principle that doesn't need to be explained every time
4. It can survive being said in jest without losing its meaning
5. It holds up next to the existing three

Test the candidate against these. If it doesn't pass, leave it in a chunk as local color. Not every good sentence belongs in the dictionary.
